PROJECT_01

Dynamic Territory Model

Automated firmographic + intent scoring engine, used by RevOps, that replaced manual account prioritization across the AE team.

STAGE_01 / IDENTIFYING THE PROBLEM

Account selection wasn't backed by data.

Doing SDR work in November 2025, I was deep-diving accounts manually — pulling 10-Ks, investor reports, LinkedIn activity, competitor usage, often paired with ChatGPT and Claude research.

Across the team, AEs and SDRs were spending ~2 hours per account on this research. It didn't scale. Worse, accounts would get worked for weeks before discovering they were clearly not a fit — wasted investment that could have been avoided with the right information up front.

The structural problem: account selection wasn't backed by data. SDRs picked randomly. AEs had hundreds of accounts assigned and weren't actively working most. A gap RevOps had flagged too.

We'd work an account for a month, then learn they'd built an in-house solution. Auto-parts hit by tariffs. Accounts in M&A. The traits that halted deals kept repeating — but nothing caught them upstream.

Built the system over Thanksgiving + Christmas to scale what I was doing manually — designed to evolve as the market and ICP change.

STAGE_02 / EXPLORING SOLUTIONS

Pitched RevOps before building.

Wanted org-wide adoption from day one. Designed the system end-to-end, wrote the scoring math, pitched a proposal deck to Head of RevOps mapping sources, data flow, and outcome goals.

The core thesis: separate fit from timing. Market signals score whether an account should ever buy. Intent signals score whether it should buy now.

The signal taxonomy wasn't from my head — validated it with AEs (what changes signal real timing) and the broader sales org (highest-intent signals that closed deals). Demoed real Claygent outputs in the pitch so RevOps could see exactly what the system would do.

Considerations

Buy vs. build: 6sense and Bombora considered as data import sources, not replacements. Their topics are generic; Squint needed manufacturing-specific signals (plant expansion, ops excellence, frontline hiring). Our ICP was still evolving — pre-built models couldn't iterate fast enough.

Why Clay: Claygents beat raw web scraping on messy signals. Clay supports embedded Python via webhooks (didn't have to give it up). And the visual table lets RevOps tune logic without me. Build for the operator.

STAGE_03 / IMPLEMENTATION

Four tables. Two scoring layers. One ranked account list.

Designed and built solo over Thanksgiving and Christmas — a Clay-based engine that imports SFDC accounts, runs three Claygents I wrote to score market signals, layers in first-party engagement, and outputs a stack-ranked list with a "why now" explanation per account.

What evolved from the proposal: the deck had Market + Intent as one combined "Table 2." In the build, I split them into 2A and 2B — separate tables — so each Claygent has its own clean schema and RevOps can tune the layers independently.

The roadblock — AI scoring drift

Same account, different runs, drifting Claygent scores. If RevOps couldn't trust the score, the system would die.

The fix is in the architecture: Table 3 snapshots + Table 4 true-flips-only + ongoing HITL spot-checks. Design for drift detection from day one — not after the system loses trust.

What I'd do differently

Clay was right for the first build — fast to ship, RevOps-tunable. A future rebuild I'd do as a modular Python + Claude system, not locked into Clay. Lets me compose AI agents across arbitrary inputs and layer in signals from my other systems (Competitor Engagement, Comp Market Map).

GTM systems only get adopted when non-engineers can tune the logic. Build for the operator, not for yourself.

PROJECT_02

Intent Signal Surfacing Layer

Three independent building blocks — company followers, competitor engagement, CEO post engagement. Each surfaces a different LinkedIn intent signal. They run in parallel.

STAGE_01 / IDENTIFYING THE PROBLEM

Warm intent was leaking past us.

From early on, Squint wasn't capturing warm signals. People engaging with Squint's company page and the CEO's LinkedIn posts — likes, follows, comments — never made it into our prospect list.

Between the company page and CEO posts, Squint was generating thousands of engagement signals from manufacturing leaders. Almost none were being prospected. Warm leads sitting in plain sight.

Hypothesis: content engagement = warm intent orders of magnitude beyond cold leads.

STAGE_02 / EXPLORING SOLUTIONS

"OpenClaw can't scrape LinkedIn."

Three options: buy Common Room/Apollo (generic, expensive), deploy OpenClaw (LinkedIn blocks autonomous agents), or build purpose-built scrapers.

The OpenClaw pitch to leadership: not "build vs. buy" — "OpenClaw wasn't built for LinkedIn." Cloudflare, behavioral detection, IP fingerprinting. Argued for purpose-built — BrightData proxies + Phantom anti-detection. The right tool for a specific surface.

Three building blocks, not one big build: built sequentially as I validated each pattern — Followers first (small + fast, booked meetings), then Competitor Engagement (deeper engineering once the pattern was proven), then CEO Post Engagement (closes the routing loop). They run as three independent pipelines.

STAGE_03 / IMPLEMENTATION

Block B deep dive — Competitor Engagement

The meatiest of the three. Deployed on Railway as two services: a Flask web app (the dashboard) and a pipeline cron service (Mondays 9am UTC). Code repo: jessalynsimonparker/competitor-tracker.

StepWhat happens
1. ScrapeBrightData hits competitor LinkedIn company pages, captures post_url, text, date, likes, comments. Trims to rolling 30-day window. Caps at 5 posts/company/run.
2. Auto-flagCompares this run's likes vs. last snapshot. Queues top 2 net-new gainers per company for Phantom. Re-queues posts that keep gaining.
3. Manual-flagDashboard route POST /api/flag/<id> flags any post for engager scraping. POST /api/posts/manual lets me paste any LinkedIn URL + pain point + poster company and queue it for Phantom.
4. Phantom workerLoops queued posts. Launches PhantomBuster "LinkedIn Post Likers Export" per URL. 3-min sleep between posts to dodge rate limits. Writes likers to Supabase profiles + engagement tables.
5. Clay enrichTwo webhooks. Profile webhook enriches likers with title/company/email/URL. Poster webhook enriches the post author (separate flow).
6. SurfaceDashboard reads from /api/posts, /api/profiles, /api/posts/<id>/likers. Pull model — no Slack notification (yet).
The honest limitation: Competitor Engagement surfaces signals via dashboard only. If a rep doesn't open it, they don't see them. That's the gap Block C closes — writeback to HubSpot/SFDC so signals reach reps where they already work.

Block C — CEO Post Engagement (current project)

# Block B — flag posts gaining engagement since last snapshot
def auto_flag_top_posts():
    for company in tracked_companies:        # competitor handles
        posts = get_recent_posts(company, days=30)
        for post in posts:
            post.delta = post.likes - post.prev_likes

        # top 2 net-new gainers per company
        top = sorted(posts, key=lambda p: p.delta, reverse=True)[:2]
        for post in top:
            if post.phantom_status != "done" or post.delta > 0:

The honest Competitor Engagement lesson

Competitor Engagement is the most technically sophisticated of the three — and it works. Sourced warm leads when running. But it was an evenings-and-weekends experiment, not yet organizationally absorbed at scale.

The lesson: GTM Engineering ships twice — once technically, once organizationally. Until a system is funded and owned by the company, it's still a personal experiment. That's the gap a GTM Engineer hire fills.

CEO Engagement is designed to be absorbable from day one — lives inside Clay + HubSpot (tools Squint already pays for). Ships by end of this week.

What it taught me

Earn the right to deeper engineering (ship lightweight to validate). Engineering shipped ≠ organizationally absorbed. Design for the budget the company already has.

CLOSING

Fit + Intent.

Territory Model decides who. Intent Surfacing Layer decides when. I'd take this same pattern to a deeper technical product and build the GTM systems that match the engineering caliber of what you're shipping.
